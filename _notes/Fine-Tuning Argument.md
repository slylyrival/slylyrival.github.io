- 2.1
	- physical improbability
		- Colyvan et al say that alternative physical laws and constants are a physical impossibility.
			- what is "the standard view of physical possibility?"
		- We would need some kind of more fundamental physical law that produce our effective laws and constants in order to say that they *could* be different in any meaningful sense.
	- logical improbability
		- If any real number is a valid value for the constants, and the probability distribution assigned to the range (-inf, inf) is uniform, then any finite interval has probability zero. Any non-uniform measure would be arbitrary and unmotivated. "life-friendly constants are highly improbably whether or not fine-tuning in the sense is required for life"
			- Isn't that last bit the point of the fine tuning argument? Yes, the right constants are highly improbable. But we ended up with them.
	- epistemic improbability
		- we knew, well before we could measure the constants, that the universe supported life. Anything we find out about the fundamental nature of the world necessarily entails that the universe supports life (as a whole, in some part of it)
		- Proponents of fine-tuning may claim that there is no clear pattern in our life-permitting combination of cosmological constants, so that combination of constants is disfavoured in terms of elegance and simplicity. The combination we have is disfavoured...but it exists...so that implies fine-tuning? That's my understanding.
- 2.2 Does improbable fine-tuning call for a response?
	- Some argue an improbable event does not require an explanation, as in a random sequence of numbers may be improbable in the set of all random sequence of numbers, but is nonetheless random. Others argue that given reasonable candidate explanations, we should not simply dismiss evaluating those hypotheses. 
		- A random sequence of numbers is *unimpressively improbable* because, if we roll a D6 ten times, we know that  whatever result we get would have a 1 in 60466176 of resulting. But fine-tuning isn't just about the improbability of the parameters - it's about the improbability of these specific parameters which result in life. If the specific D6 sequence we get result in a rabbit being pulled out of a hat, it would be more interesting than just any other sequence.
	- Juhl argues that because life is causally ramified, we should expect it to depend sensitively on potentially relevant parameters. 
		- I'm not sure whether would should expect that or not. But even still, say that given life exists, it is utterly expected that the situation that life arises in is narrowly tailored to life. That seems to me to cry out for moving back one more step and wondering and asking why we got so lucky.
		- Are these guys really arguing that it doesn't need a response? Or just that it doesn't *require* a response? What does it even me to call or not call for a response? 
		-  [ ] Check out the Juhl and Gould and Carlson and Olssen to see what they are really saying.
- 2.3 Avoiding Fine-Tuning for Life Through New Physics?
	- Similarly to how evolution answers the question of why the world and its biological organisms seem so complex and "designed," there may be a future where the fundamentals of physics are discovered which explain life friendly conditions. Einstein posits an idea that "the laws and constants of physics will turn out to be dictated completely by fundamental principles." (quote from SEP, not einstein).
		- I don't really see how eliminating alternate possible values for the laws and constants answer's the mail. If the laws and constants couldn't be anything BUT the life-friendly constants we know and love, isn't that even more indication that some entity picked those laws and constants specifically?
	- String theory predicts that there could be different laws and constants, so the advances in physics are not leaning towards Einstein's idea anyway.
	- Inflationary cosmology may explain the fine tuning of the critical energy density of the universe. It does not, however, explain the fine tuning of the other laws and constants.
- 3.1 The Argument from Fine-Tuning for Design Using Probabilities
	- Re: P(R | D) ~= 1. Should we really expect the current situation with close to 1 probability only given D, where D is there a cosmic designer who desires to bring about life? Couldn't it have happened myriad other ways that would not have seemed finely tuned?
		- SEP says R is only "that the constants are right for life," not that they are finely tuned, any deviation from which would be inhospitable to life
	- It seems nigh impossible to assign prior probabilities in any rational way.

# Random Thoughts
- A weakness of this argument is that it relies on very technical foundations that are not verifiable or tangible to the audience. Statements like "Had [the strong nuclear force] been 50% larger, almost all hydrogen would have been burned in the very early universe" just do not hit as hard as the image of a causal chain having no beginning.
- Weak nuclear force not necessary for life - would we expect a fine-tuned universe to have extraneous fundamentals?
