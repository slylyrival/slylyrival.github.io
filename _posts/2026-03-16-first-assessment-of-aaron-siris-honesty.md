# The Impetus

Given that was going to have a child in the coming months, and given the increased conversation around vaccine safety in the United States over the last few years, and the last year especially, I took it upon myself to do my due diligence, as best I could given the time and intelligence allotted to me, to look at the claims of certain individuals and try to understand their positions.

**Initial Bias**: Due to a variety of political, social, and cultural media I have settled into consuming, my bias is towards trusting the establishment narrative surrounding the safety of vaccines.

Aaron Siri has become a prominent spokesperson for the idea that the vaccine's have not been properly studied to determine their true safety profile and that the United State's vaccine policy is excessive and corrupt given the inability to sue vaccine manufacturers.

Last fall, I watched the September 2025 ACIP meeting in which the committee discussed and debated the policy of recommending a universal Hepatitis-B vaccine dose at birth. I followed the meeting as best I could and afterwards sought out some voices from vaccine safety skeptics to see how they felt about claims made at the meeting. I saw one tweet from Aaron Siri that made a concrete claim about what the staff scientists at the CDC presented regarding the HepB vaccine and decided to investigate Siri's claim further to make an initial assessment of his trustworthiness on the topic.

The study linked is [“Hepatitis B vaccination associated with higher female than male mortality in Guinea-bissau: an observational study.”](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15626943/) by Garly et al (2004). 

The question's I aim to answer are:

1. Did Garly (2004) find that the HepB vaccine increased deaths?
2. If so, did the CDC presentation fail to make that clear?

# Investigation

## Did Garly (2004) find that the HepB vaccine increased deaths?

Garly (2004) found that the mortality ratio (MRR) between 7.5-12 month and 1.5-7.5 month age groups  for Hepatitis-B vaccine (HBV) HBV-unvaccinated cohorts was 0.97 (95% CI, 0.77-1.23) compared to 1.62 for the HBV vaccinated cohorts. 

Among children in the measles vaccine (MV_ trial, the MRR for HBV to non-HBV children 7.5-12 months was 1.81 (95% CI 1.19, 2.75).

Female-to-male MRR was 1.66 (95% CI 0.80-3.45) in HBV+MV children through 12 months. Female-to-male MRR was 2.20 (95% CI 1.07, 4.54) in HBV+MV children through 24 months.
Female-to-male MRR was 0.95 (95% CI 0.70, 1.32) in MV only children through 24 months.

Does any of this mean that “HepB vaccine increased deaths?” No.

The study relied on offering the HBV to children who were already enrolled in a measles RCT. The HBV was not given randomly. Parents of children who are more sickly may be more likely to accept the offer for the HBV vaccine and thus drive up the mortality rate. It is wrong to claim that the study shows that “HepB vaccine increased deaths.”

## If so, did the CDC presentation fail to make that clear?

As stated above, Garly (2004) did not find that the HepB vaccine increased deaths. Even so, I will investigate if the CDC failed to adequately address the mortality effects seen in Garly (2004). Siri follows his claim up with the following quote from the article abstract: “HBV-vaccinated children 7 ½-12 months of age had higher mortality than both prior and subsequent cohorts who had not received HBV (MR=1.81; 95% CI 1.19-2.75).”

The CDC presented the results for Garly (2004) in their presentation [“Non-specific effects following hepatitis B vaccination” given on September 18, 2025](https://www.cdc.gov/acip/downloads/slides-2025-09-18-19/03-su-hep-b-508.pdf). Slide 9 presents the results for the paper.

The result that Siri claimed the CDC failed to present is present under the Comparison 2 bullet on slide 9 of the presentation. Additionally, reviewing the video footage from the ACIP meeting shows that Dr. Su from the CDC stated this exact result at [5:28:14 into the video](https://youtu.be/-6uBNXsYRHQ?t=19694).

# Conclusion

The CDC adequately and completely presented the results of the Garly paper, and in fact presented them with much more context about the limitations of interpreting those conclusions than the short summary a reader would get from Siri’s tweet.

# Interpreting this Investigation

This is an investigation of a single easily-checkable claim that Aaron Siri made. It does not mean that all other claims made by him are so completely wrong. Given that I did not know beforehand how completely wrong he was going to be, I would update downwards on an estimation of Siri's trustworthiness.
