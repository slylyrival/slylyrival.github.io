title: "Blaise Pascal thinks it's okay to doomscroll"
date: 2026-04-09

> *"I have discovered that all the unhappiness of men arises from one single fact, that they cannot stay quietly in their own chamber."* - Blaise Pascal, Pensees 139

*(All quotations in this post sourced from Project Gutenberg's [publication](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/18269/18269-h/18269-h.htm) of the Pensees)*

You have probably heard the above quote, in some form or another. As of this writing the quote has 2345 likes on [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/19682-all-of-humanity-s-problems-stem-from-man-s-inability-to-sit). It's the kind of quote perfectly designed for me to read and nod solemnly to myself, muttering "so true." It's the damn phones. It's the loud music in public. It's Netflix and Hulu and TikTok and Instgram -- everybody these days just hurries hurries hurries and never stops to enjoy some peace and quiet!

I had the idea for this post a few weeks ago. Some [comments on a post](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47698802) on HackerNews today of an [article titled "The Importance of Being Idle."](https://theamericanscholar.org/the-importance-of-being-idle/) brought it back to mind. The article discusses the importance of being truly idle, doing nothing: no distractions, no activities, no heightened emotions of any sort. Just pure being. A commenter on HN raises the Pascal quote and calling idleness a "noble pursuit." Further comments note that digital technology has "exacerbated the problem," making it more difficult to sit quietly alone in a room.

This is not an uncommon interpretation of Pascal. When you Google the quotation, a top result is this [Medium post ](https://medium.com/swlh/sitting-in-a-room-alone-why-pascal-was-spot-on-87cfac8af5d3) by a fellow named J Clay. He writes:

> Being reasonably observant, what struck me quite early on in my time in London was the sheer scale of mindless distraction that people require. What do I mean by this? One can’t help but notice the people around you and what they’re doing. And what they’re doing is everything they can to distract themselves. I mean, I could excuse a lot of this if they were doing things to grow themselves like reading intelligent books, but this is rarely the case. No, they’re on their phones scrolling. They’re playing mindless Tetris-type phone games. They’re watching some TV programme. They have earphones stuck in their ears with strains of loud beats audible to you next to them. Or they’re having long, mindless conversations. The types where you start wondering whether there is in fact anyone on the other end of the line.

Clay continues in this fashion, patting himself on the back for enjoying birdsong on his walks instead of mindlessly distracting himself with slop. (Later on, he tells us he is "an introvert who doesn't need that much conversation in daily life. Certainly not of the mundane type." We should all be so lucky to be rid of our troublesome need for mundane conversation.) He concludes by saying Pascal's advice about sitting quietly in a room is good, and that mindfulness and meditation are ancient concepts we should practice.

Unfortunately, it seems to me that Pascal did not have any form of mindfulness and meditation in mind.

Pascal's point is that we need the diversion to be happy. He does not say that if only people practiced meditation they would be able to sit in a room quietly and thus solve humanity's problems. Rather, he says we are simply unable to do so. Completely unable. And this being the case, we must distract ourselves from this restful state. A restful state only reveals our unhappy state in life:

> "When we imagine a king attended with every pleasure he can feel, if he be without diversion, and be left to consider and reflect on what he is, this feeble happiness will not sustain him; he will necessarily fall into forebodings of dangers, of revolutions which may happen, and, finally, of death and inevitable disease."

His solution: distract yourself! Doomscroll! BUT - do it with the knowledge that your happiness also does not lie in the goal of your diversion, but in the diversion itself:

> "This is all that men have been able to discover to make themselves happy. And those who philosophise on the matter, and who think men unreasonable for spending a whole day in chasing a hare which they would not have bought, scarce know our nature. The hare in itself would not screen us from the sight of death and calamities; but *the chase which turns away our attention from these, does screen us*."

Pascal recognizes clearly the First Noble Truth: Life involves unsatisfactoriness. He also recognizes the Second Noble Truth, that unsatisfactoriness comes from desires - desires which will not be satisfied when their objects are gained: "Man...[plays] every day for a small stake. Give him each morning the money he can win each day, on condition he does not play; you make him miserable." He and Mr. Gautama would disagree on Truth 3 and 4, however, which say (3) the cessation of the craving is what ceases suffering and (4) that the Noble Eightfold Path, which includes Right Mindfulness, is the method to the cessation of suffering.

The modern references to Pascal's quote regularly bring it up in the context of meditation and other mindfulness practices. I find these practices tremendously helpful. They allow me to stop cycles of unhappy thought, recognize the reality of situation around me instead of the negative perceptions brought on by the runaway train of my thoughts. And I can do so without having to chase any hares. But this is clearly not what Pascal had in mind. He thought diversion is entirely appropriate for the unhappy ruminations occasioned by reflections on one's existence that may occur in quiet rooms. By all means, meditate, be mindful, sit quietly in as many rooms as you can. Pascal thinks you're wasting your time.
